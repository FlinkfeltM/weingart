Wolfgang Weingart Placement holder

Prologue.

Weingart was born February 1941, near the Swiss border in southern Germany, Salem Valley. He grew up during the end of the second world war. According to Weingart himself, his childhood was the most important part of his life, even though it was at the end of a prolonged war, it was at this time that he realized that he was more of a doer than a thinker.

Weingart was never fond of the typical education given to him; the idea of cramming information into his head didn’t appeal to him. With various creative methods, he cheated his ways throughout school, from drilling holes into his desk, reading the answers to making a battery driven device to tell him the anticipated answers written and placed into the same device the night before.

At the age of 13, in 1954 he moved to Lisbon with his mother for a brief period of time, only to return to Germany four years later in 1958. After his return to Germany he enrolled in Merz Academy in Stuttgart, where he attended a two-year programme in graphic arts. During these two years at Merz Academy he developed certain skills including typesetting, linocut and woodblock printing.

After his time at Merx Academy, Weingart began his typesetting apprenticeship in hot metal hand composition at Ruwe Printing, where he came into contact with the designer Karl-August Hanke, who was the consulting designer of the company. Hanke became Weingart’s mentor, encouraging him to continue his studies in Switzerland.

At the beginning of his three year typesetting apprenticeship, Weingart became fascinated with Swiss typography and graphic design. In 1963, he arranged a visit to Basel, to meet with Armin Hofmann with hopes of getting accepted into the Basel School of Design. In a turn of events, during the interview when Weingart was showing Hofmann his works, he was asked if he would consider teaching in his Graphics design programme. A year later Weingart moved to Basel and enrolled in the school as an independent student. In 1968 he got hired to teach typography in the advanced class for graphic design at the Basel School of Design.

"Having been an early admirer of the beauty of letters, I became insensibly desirous of contributing to the perfection of them. I formed to myself ideas of greater accuracy than had yet appeared, and had endeavoured to produce a set of types according to what I conceived to be their true proportion."
-John Baskerville, preface to Milton, 1758 (Anatomy of a Typeface)
